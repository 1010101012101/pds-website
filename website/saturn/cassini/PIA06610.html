--- layout: base layout_style: default title: PIA06610: Saturn at a Tilt --- # PIA06610: Saturn at a Tilt [ ![Saturn at a Tilt](browse/PIA06610.jpg)](jpegMod/PIA06610_modest.jpg) [Medium-Res JPEG](jpegMod/PIA06610_modest.jpg) [Full-Res JPEG](//photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/jpeg/PIA06610.jpg) (68.15 kB) [Full-Res TIFF](//photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/tiff/PIA06610.tif) (721.5 kB) **Caption:** Saturn's whirling vortices and feathery cloud bands are the signs of a restless world. Cassini captured this arresting view of the giant planet scored by bold shadows cast by the rings. The rings are seen edge-on in this dramatic, artfully tilted scene. The image was taken with the Cassini spacecraft wide-angle camera on March 6, 2005, at a distance of approximately 1.7 million kilometers (1 million miles) from Saturn through a filter sensitive to wavelengths of infrared light centered at 750 nanometers. The image scale is 10 kilometers (6 miles) per pixel. The Cassini-Huygens mission is a cooperative project of NASA, the European Space Agency and the Italian Space Agency. The Jet Propulsion Laboratory, a division of the California Institute of Technology in Pasadena, manages the mission for NASA's Science Mission Directorate, Washington, D.C. The Cassini orbiter and its two onboard cameras were designed, developed and assembled at JPL. The imaging team is based at the Space Science Institute, Boulder, Colo.
