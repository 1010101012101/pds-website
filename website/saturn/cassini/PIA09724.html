--- layout: base layout_style: default title: "PIA09724: Moonwatching" --- # PIA09724: Moonwatching [ ![ Moonwatching](browse/PIA09724.jpg)](jpegMod/PIA09724_modest.jpg) [Medium-Res JPEG](jpegMod/PIA09724_modest.jpg) [Full-Res JPEG](//photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/jpeg/PIA09724.jpg) (17.1 kB) [Full-Res TIFF](//photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/tiff/PIA09724.tif) (258.6 kB) **Caption:** Two of Saturn's moons make appearances in this view in very different ways. Janus (181 kilometers, or 113 miles across) glides past at bottom, near the edge-on ringplane. Above are the arcing shadows cast onto the northern hemisphere by the rings, along with the shadow of Mimas (397 kilometers, or 247 miles across) against a backdrop of wispy clouds. Mimas' shadow appears elliptical due to its projection onto the spheroidal shape of Saturn's visible atmosphere. The image was taken with the Cassini spacecraft wide-angle camera on Aug. 5, 2007 using a spectral filter sensitive to wavelengths of infrared light centered at 750 nanometers. The view was acquired at a distance of approximately 4 million kilometers (2.5 million miles) from Saturn. Image scale is 47 kilometers (29 miles) per pixel.
