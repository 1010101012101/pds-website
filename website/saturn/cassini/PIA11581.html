--- layout: base layout_style: default title: "PIA11581: Pan's Lengthening Shadow" --- # PIA11581: Pan's Lengthening Shadow [ ![ Pan's Lengthening Shadow](browse/PIA11581.jpg)](jpegMod/PIA11581_modest.jpg) [Medium-Res JPEG](jpegMod/PIA11581_modest.jpg) [Full-Res JPEG](//photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/jpeg/PIA11581.jpg) (68.1 kB) [Full-Res TIFF](//photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/tiff/PIA11581.tif) (1.042 MB) **Caption:** Saturn's moon Pan casts a longer shadow across the A ring as the planet's August 2009 equinox draws near. For an earlier image showing a shorter shadow, see [PIA116512](PIA11652.html). The novel illumination geometry created around the time of Saturn's August 2009 equinox allows moons orbiting in or near the plane of Saturn's equatorial rings to cast shadows onto the rings. These scenes are possible only during the few months before and after Saturn's equinox, which occurs only once in about 15 Earth years. To learn more about this special time and to see movies of moons' shadows moving across the rings,see [PIA11651](PIA11651.html) and [PIA11660](PIA11660.html). Pan (28 kilometers, or 17 miles across) orbits in the Encke Gap. This view looks toward the unilluminated side of the rings from about 34 degrees above the ringplane. The image was taken in visible light with the Cassini spacecraft narrow-angle camera on July 27, 2009. The view was acquired at a distance of approximately 839,000 kilometers (521,000 miles) from Pan and at a Sun-Pan-spacecraft, or phase, angle of 74 degrees. Image scale is 5 kilometers (3 miles) per pixel. The Cassini-Huygens mission is a cooperative project of NASA, the European Space Agency and the Italian Space Agency.
