# Pan's Lengthening Shadow * Click the [image above](/press_releases/medium/PIA11xxx/PIA11581_med.jpg) for a larger view * [Full-Res JPEG](https://photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/jpeg/PIA11581.jpg) (1020 x 1020) (68.1 kB) * [Full-Res TIFF](https://photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/tiff/PIA11581.tif) (1020 x 1020) (1.0 MB)

Caption:

Saturn's moon Pan casts a longer shadow across the A ring as the planet's August 2009 equinox draws near.

For an earlier image showing a shorter shadow, see PIA11652

The novel illumination geometry created around the time of Saturn's August 2009 equinox allows moons orbiting in or near the plane of Saturn's equatorial rings to cast shadows onto the rings. These scenes are possible only during the few months before and after Saturn's equinox, which occurs only once in about 15 Earth years. To learn more about this special time and to see movies of moons' shadows moving across the rings,see PIA11651 and PIA11660 .

Pan (28 kilometers, or 17 miles across) orbits in the Encke Gap. This view looks toward the unilluminated side of the rings from about 34 degrees above the ringplane.

The image was taken in visible light with the Cassini spacecraft narrow-angle camera on July 27, 2009. The view was acquired at a distance of approximately 839,000 kilometers (521,000 miles) from Pan and at a Sun-Pan-spacecraft, or phase, angle of 74 degrees. Image scale is 5 kilometers (3 miles) per pixel.
