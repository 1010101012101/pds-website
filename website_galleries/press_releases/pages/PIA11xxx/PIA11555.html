# Cutting 'Cross the C Ring * Click the [image above](/press_releases/medium/PIA11xxx/PIA11555_med.jpg) for a larger view * [Full-Res JPEG](https://photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/jpeg/PIA11555.jpg) (1016 x 1016) (80.5 kB) * [Full-Res TIFF](https://photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/tiff/PIA11555.tif) (1016 x 1016) (1.0 MB)

Caption:

The shadow of the moon Tethys cuts across the C ring in this image taken as Saturn approaches its August 2009 equinox.

The novel illumination geometry created as Saturn approaches its August 2009 equinox allows moons orbiting in or near the plane of Saturn's equatorial rings to cast shadows onto the rings. These scenes are possible only during the few months before and after Saturn's equinox, which occurs only once in about 15 Earth years. To learn more about this special time and to see movies of moons' shadows moving across the rings, see PIA11651 and PIA11660 .

Saturn, although very faint, can be seen lit by ringshine on the right of the image. Stars can be seen through the rings and in the background.

This view looks toward the sunlit side of the rings from about 36 degrees below the ringplane. The image was taken in visible light with the Cassini spacecraft wide-angle camera on June 9, 2009. The view was obtained at a distance of approximately 578,000 kilometers (359,000 miles) from Saturn and at a Sun-Saturn-spacecraft, or phase, angle of 66 degrees. Image scale is 31 kilometers (19 miles) per pixel.
